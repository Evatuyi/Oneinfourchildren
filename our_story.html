About One In Four Children

Who We Are


History tells us that the Nuba Mountains has experienced prolonged periods of economic and political marginalization, racial discrimination, cultural suppression, conflict and displacement.

One in Four Children believes that communities build communities. We work Private organisations, school communities and government organisations to rebuild the Nuba Mountains through Education, Empowerment and Capacity Building programs.At the heart of what we do is to ensure no child is left without education because of the misfortune of their circumstances

Our Story

One in Four Children is Founded by Stephen Kamal, a child of war and former refugee. Stephen left the Nuba Mountains in 1996 after the intensely horrific war, drought and engineered famine that resulted in an estimated 100,000 to 200,000 deaths of the Nuba People. Stephen see’s education as a pillar of society. The Catalyst for One in Four Children were the many conversations with Nuba children in Refugee camps living in Uganda, Sudan, Egypt and Kenya, in which many students said lack of quality books was a major pain point for them. Along with Peter Sambia Kagi Kowa, they set out to change the narrative for so many of these children stuck in a complete cycle of poverty. We believe books change lives. We know because we’ve lived on both sides of the fence and the difference some literacy in the way of being able to read and write can make is immeasurable.


What We Do


Our Work In Australia

The In Australia component of work involves advocating for integration, and promoting diversity and inclusion programs for African youths aimed at removing barriers to entry into institutions of higher learning. We do this through in collaboration with industry, African youths and community groups through participation in and facilitation of culturally sensitive training, employment initiatives, and education-based workshops, events and discourse.

We seek to inspire African Youth to pursue higher education because education opens doors to meaningful employment


Our In Australia Work Supporting Overseas Operations

  • Fundraising costs to cover shipping to Africa
  • Liaising with community members and organisations to source books and other school materials
  • Organising collection, sorting and Storage of books and school materials
  • Coordinating logistics efforts to send the books to Sudan
  • Working with Local Authorities to get customs clearance for the books as they arrive at port
  • Coordinating volunteer team members across Kenya, Uganda and Sudan to ensure books arrive as intended
  • Sharing our learning through publications, workshops, events to raise awareness of literacy levels in the Nuba mountains and across Africa
  • Sharing and strengthening Nuba culture, identity and history with the Australian community
  • Advocating for stronger mental health awareness and support for Sudanese youth to promote entry to higher education

In South Sudan and in the Nuba Mountains

  • Securing safe passage for team members transporting books
  • Organising storage for books and school materials
  • Prioritizing distribution of books and materials
  • Collaborating with school communities to set-up school libraries
  • Organising transportation of books to schools in a rotational loan scheme
  • Facilitating Capacity building programs and literacy training and workshops for adults in the community
  • Promoting peer led reading programs in schools
  • Working in collaboration with government to promote Female Empowerment Programs

The People We Serve


The Nuba Mountains

The People of the Nuba Mountains is a region in Sudan that continues to experience pervasive poverty, War trauma, lack basic health care services and poor nutrition. Education is the only way out. Every family has lived the experience of the war, many children are either orphaned or supported by single parents most of whom are mothers. Children especially young girls have been forced to remain at home to take care of their family, walking for days on end in search of water and food to eat the family, they are also tasked with general household duties which make it difficult to attain an education. For every girl educated, 7 more people benefit. The aim of One in Four is to promote equal opportunity and access to quality education for all children of the Nuba Mountains and Africa as a continent. We aim to build thriving, sustainable and self reliant communities wherever we work through Education initiatives that promote attainment of knowledge for all who seek.


The Nuba Mountains, A brief History


  1. February 1993: More than 75,000 Nuba women and children are enslaved, children raped and massacred, civilians executed

  2. March 1993: Number of Nuba people facing ethnic cleaning approaches 1000,000 people

  3. July 1995: The African Rights Group (ARG)reports massive human rights violations, including murder, rape, and the destruction of churches and mosques.

  4. February 1996: U.N. Rapporteur on Human Rights Gaspar Biro asserts that “in the Nuba Mountains, a large number of civilians, including women and children, Muslims and Christians alike, have been killed in [aerial] attacks or summarily executed.” It is estimated that there are only 250–300 thousand Nuba left in SPLA-administered regions while at least 500 thousand have been forcibly resettled by the GoS in so-called “peace camps.” It is also estimated that, at a minimum, one hundred thousand, and possibly as many as two hundred thousand, Nuba have been forced from their villages and farms since 1983.

  5. October 26, 1997: GOS sponsored militias commit murder, rape, forced displacement, incarceration in so-called “peace camps,” slavery, sexual slavery, planned famines, the purposeful destruction of food, overt blocking of humanitarian aid, and forced conversions to Islam.

  6. December 1998: More than 2 hundred thousand Nuba people are said to have been killed in the war

  7. June 7, 2011: Another 60000, civilians are displaced by intense fighting between SPLM/N and GOS

  8. June 8, 2011: SAF reports it wants to “clear the state of the remaining rebels, Nuba people.”

  9. June 30, 2011: Ten Nuba towns and villages in South Kordofan are bombed and wiped out.

  10. July 5, 2011: Of 141 schools 100 schools close, 20,000 students without school

  11. September 27, 2011: More than 25,000 refugees flee to Ethiopia for refuge

  12. October 5, 2011: Food crisis strikes, Hundreds of thousands are without food

  13. December 2014: More than 450 bombs, rockets, and artillery are dropped on the Nuba Mountains
